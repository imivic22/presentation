Coordination & Subordination

Tools for Shaping Meaning in Writing

"Coordination and subordination control emphasis, rhythm, clarity, and persuasion. Skilled writers choose between them based on purpose, not habit."

Today's Focus:

  • Understanding coordination and subordination
  • Learning when to use each one
  • Mastering parallel structure
  • Avoiding common mistakes

What is Coordination?

Coordination joins elements of equal importance.

The Coordinating Conjunctions: FANBOYS

  • For
  • And
  • Nor
  • But
  • Or
  • Yet
  • So
I wanted to go to the party, but I had too much homework.
Key Idea: Coordination says "these ideas matter equally."

What Can Be Coordinated?

1. Words

She is smart and funny.

2. Phrases

You can take a taxi or a shuttle from the airport.

3. Independent Clauses (Complete Sentences)

The sun was setting, and the sky turned orange.
Important: When joining two independent clauses, use a comma before the FANBOY.

Parallel Structure

Making Coordination Sound Professional

Parallelism: Coordinated elements must share the same grammatical form.

PARALLEL: She likes reading, writing, and editing.
NOT PARALLEL: She likes reading, to write, and editing.

More Parallel Structure Examples:

PARALLEL (all adjectives): The professor is known for being clear, humorous, and patient.
NOT PARALLEL (mixed forms): The professor is known for clarity, being humorous, and patience.

Why Parallel Structure Matters

It Affects How Your Writing Sounds:

AWKWARD: The goals are to increase sales, improving customer service, and we should reduce costs.
SMOOTH: The goals are to increase sales, to improve customer service, and to reduce costs.
OR: The goals are increasing sales, improving customer service, and reducing costs.

Why Readers Notice:

  • Sounds cleaner and more professional
  • Easier for the brain to process
  • Creates rhythm and flow
  • Strengthens your credibility as a writer
Readers can feel when something isn't parallel—even if they can't explain why it sounds wrong.

What is Subordination?

Subordination makes one idea dependent on another, creating a hierarchy of importance.

Key Difference:

Coordination Subordination
Ideas are equal One idea is more important
Both parts can stand alone One part depends on the other
Feels balanced Shows relationships (cause, time, condition)
Writer's Power: Subordination lets you decide what deserves center stage.

Common Subordinating Conjunctions

Time

  • when
  • while
  • after
  • before

Cause

  • because
  • since
  • as

Condition

  • if
  • unless

Contrast

  • although
  • though
  • while
Each word signals a specific relationship: Choose carefully because the subordinator you pick changes the meaning!

Subordination in Action

Dependent Clause First (use comma)

Because it was raining, we stayed inside.

Independent Clause First (no comma needed)

We stayed inside because it was raining.
Comma Rule: When the dependent clause comes FIRST, use a comma. When it comes SECOND, no comma is needed.

More Examples:

Although she studied hard, she was nervous about the test.
I'll go to the gym if I finish my work early.

Understanding the Nuance

When Both Coordination and Subordination Work

Sometimes both structures are grammatically correct, but they mean slightly different things:

Coordination (with "so"): The sun was shining, so we went to the beach.
Meaning: Both facts are presented equally; "so" shows result but doesn't emphasize cause as strongly.
Subordination (with "because"): Because the sun was shining, we went to the beach.
Meaning: The cause (shining sun) is clearly the reason for the effect (going to beach).
The Difference:
• "So" (coordination) = presents both ideas as equally important facts, shows a result
• "Because" (subordination) = explicitly shows cause and effect, emphasizes the reasoning

Choose based on what you want to emphasize!

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Comma Splices

WRONG: I love pizza, it's delicious.
RIGHT: I love pizza, and it's delicious.

2. Sentence Fragments

WRONG: Because I was tired.
RIGHT: Because I was tired, I went to bed early.

3. Missing Commas

WRONG: Although the test was hard we all passed.
RIGHT: Although the test was hard, we all passed.

More Common Mistakes

4. Breaking Parallelism

WRONG: She likes reading, to write, and editing.
RIGHT: She likes reading, writing, and editing.

5. Overusing Coordination

WRONG: I woke up and I was tired and I drank coffee and I felt better.
RIGHT: Although I woke up tired, I drank coffee and felt better.
Remember: Punctuation reflects structure—it doesn't create it. You need the right conjunction, not just commas!

Quick Review

Coordination (FANBOYS)

  • Joins equal ideas
  • Use comma before FANBOY when joining independent clauses
  • Keep parallel structure!

Subordination

  • Creates hierarchy of ideas
  • Shows relationships: cause, time, condition, contrast
  • Comma when dependent clause comes first
Choose based on your purpose: What relationship between ideas do you want to show? What do you want to emphasize?

Activity 1: Identify It!

For each sentence, identify if it uses COORDINATION or SUBORDINATION:

  1. I wanted to go hiking, but it started raining.
  2. When the movie ended, everyone applauded.
  3. She studies hard, so she gets good grades.
  4. Although he was nervous, he gave a great presentation.

Activity 1: Answers

  1. I wanted to go hiking, but it started raining. → COORDINATION
  2. When the movie ended, everyone applauded. → SUBORDINATION
  3. She studies hard, so she gets good grades. → COORDINATION
  4. Although he was nervous, he gave a great presentation. → SUBORDINATION
  5. The team practiced daily, and they won the championship. → COORDINATION

Activity 2: Fix the Errors!

Each sentence has an error. Can you spot it and fix it?

  1. I love reading, it helps me relax.
  2. Because she was sick.
  3. Although the test was hard we all passed.
  4. She enjoys swimming, running, and to bike.
  5. The concert was canceled the singer was ill.

Activity 2: Answers

1. I love reading, it helps me relax. (Comma splice)

FIX: I love reading, and it helps me relax.
OR: I love reading because it helps me relax.

2. Because she was sick. (Fragment)

FIX: Because she was sick, she stayed home.

3. Although the test was hard we all passed. (Missing comma)

FIX: Although the test was hard, we all passed.

4. She enjoys swimming, running, and to bike. (Not parallel)

FIX: She enjoys swimming, running, and biking.

5. The concert was canceled the singer was ill. (Run-on)

FIX: The concert was canceled because the singer was ill.

Activity 3: Your Turn!

Combine these simple sentences. You choose: use COORDINATION or SUBORDINATION!

1. The sun was shining. We decided to go to the beach.

2. I studied all night. I still failed the test.

3. She loves coffee. She drinks it every morning.

4. The power went out. We lit candles.

Activity 3: Sample Answers

Many answers are possible! Here are some good options:

1. The sun was shining. We decided to go to the beach.

The sun was shining, and we decided to go to the beach. (coordination - both facts equal)
Because the sun was shining, we decided to go to the beach. (subordination - shows cause)

Note: "So" would also work for coordination and shows the result, but doesn't emphasize cause/effect as strongly as "because."

2. I studied all night. I still failed the test.

I studied all night, but I still failed the test. (coordination - contrast)
Although I studied all night, I still failed the test. (subordination - emphasizes the surprising result)

3. She loves coffee. She drinks it every morning.

She loves coffee, and she drinks it every morning. (coordination - two equal facts)
She drinks coffee every morning because she loves it. (subordination - shows reason)

4. The power went out. We lit candles.

The power went out, so we lit candles. (coordination - shows result)
When the power went out, we lit candles. (subordination - shows time/sequence)

Key Takeaways

  1. Coordination (FANBOYS) joins equal ideas—use it when both ideas matter equally.
  2. Subordination creates hierarchy—use it to show cause/effect, time, conditions, or contrasts.
  3. Parallel structure makes your writing sound professional and polished.
  4. Comma rules matter: Use commas correctly with both coordination and subordination.
  5. Choose purposefully: The structure you pick shapes how readers understand your ideas.
"Good writing is about structure as much as content."
